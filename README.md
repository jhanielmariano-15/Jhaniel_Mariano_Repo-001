# Human Computer Interaction &amp; User-Centered Design Research

## 3 Major Sources on Human-Computer Interaction (HCI)

*If you’re looking into how people and technology work together, these three sources often cited in academic and professional research regarding HCI.*

### 1. The Interaction Design Foundation (IxDF)
**-** Think of this as the ultimate library for design. They break down HCI not just as a technical field, but as a mix of psychology and engineering. It's about understanding the "why" behind how people click and swipe.

**Source:** [What is Human-Computer Interaction? (IxDF)](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/human-computer-interaction)

### 2. Nielsen Norman Group (NN/g)
**-** Founded by Don Norman and Jakob Nielsen, These are the pioneers of usability. They created the "10 Heuristics," which are basically the ten commandments for making software that doesn't frustrate people. If a website is easy to use, they probably influenced it. 

**Source:** [10 Usability Heuristics for UI Design](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ten-usability-heuristics/)

### 3. The Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) - SIGCHI
**-** This is where the heavy-duty academic research happens. It’s a global community of experts who study the future of tech, everything from AI to how we’ll interact with computers 10 years from now.

**Source:** [ACM SIGCHI (Special Interest Group on Computer-Human Interaction)](https://sigchi.org/)


## What are the core focus of HCI?
*When we talk about the focus of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), we aren't just talking about making buttons look pretty. We are talking about the "bridge" between human thought and digital action.*

HCI focuses on making technology feel like a natural extension of ourselves rather than a confusing tool we have to struggle to understand.


## The 4 Pillars of HCI Focus
**-** To keep things simple, HCI focuses on the "Interaction Loop" between four specific areas:

#### 1. The Human (The "Who")
**-** Instead of forcing people to act like computers, HCI studies how humans think, see, and feel. It looks at our cognitive load (how much information we can handle at once) and our physical limits.

**Focus:** Empathy, psychology, and physical ergonomics.

#### 2. The Computer (The "What")
**-** This involves the technology itself,not just laptops, but everything from smartwatches to the self-checkout machine at the grocery store.

**Focus:** Input devices (voice, touch, gesture) and output displays that are easy to read and interpret.

#### 3. The Task (The "Why")
**-** HCI experts look at what the user is actually trying to do. Is the user trying to save a life in a hospital, or just trying to send a funny meme to a friend?

**Focus:** Efficiency and making the path to the goal as short and painless as possible.

#### 4. The Context (The "Where")
**-** A banking app shouldn't be designed the same way as a fitness app used while running. HCI focuses on the environment—whether it’s a noisy construction site, a dark bedroom, or a moving car.

**Focus:** Environmental factors and social norms.

## II. 5 User-Centered Design (UCD) Principles
**-** User-Centered Design is an iterative process where the user's needs focus at every stage.

A. **Empathy and User Understanding**
**-** Design must be based on an explicit understanding of users, tasks, and environments. You are not designing for yourself; you are designing for them.

B. **Iterative Design (Repetition)**
**-** The design is driven and refined by user-centered evaluation. You design, test, fix, and test again. It is a cycle, not a straight line.

C. **Consistency**
**-** Interface elements (colors, fonts, button behaviors) must be consistent throughout the application to lower the learning curve for the user.

D. **Feedback and Visibility**
**-** The system should always keep users informed about what is going on (e.g., loading spinners, success messages) within a reasonable amount of time.

E. **Accessibility (Inclusivity)**
**-** Applications must be usable by people with a wide range of abilities and disabilities (e.g., ensuring text has high contrast for visually impaired users).

**Reference**: [ISO 9241-210 Standard](https://www.iso.org/standard/77520.html) and [Usability.gov](https://www.usability.gov/what-and-why/user-centered-design.html)


## III. Considerations in Developing User-Centered Applications
**-** When moving from design to software development, these technical considerations ensure the app remains user-friendly.

### 1. Usability and Learnability
**Consideration:** How easy is it for users to accomplish basic tasks the first time? Navigation must be intuitive so users do not need a manual.

### 2. Context of Use (Environment)
**Consideration:** Where will the software be used?

**Example:** If it is a navigation app for drivers, buttons must be large and text minimal to ensure safety while driving.

### 3. Performance and Latency
**Consideration:** A user-centered app respects the user's time. If software lags or takes more than 2-3 seconds to load, users may abandon it.

### 4. Error Prevention and Recovery
**Consideration:** The software should prevent problems (e.g., "Are you sure you want to delete?"). If errors happen, messages should be in plain language, not error codes.

### 5. Data Privacy and Trust
**Consideration:** Users must feel safe. The application must clearly state how data is used and ensure personal information is encrypted.
